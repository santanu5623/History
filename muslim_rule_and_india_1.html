The Politics of Historical Memory in India

An examination of religious and political conflict in the context of pre-modern Indo-Islamic statecraft.

Published on 2025-10-19 by Nityaeanjan Das | Translator: Santanu Mandal

I. Introduction: Setting the Stage

The historical period spanning the Delhi Sultanate and the Mughal Empire is viewed by many early 20th-century historians, such as **R. C. Majumdar and Jadunath Sarkar**, as a prolonged era defined by military conquest and religious strife. For these scholars, the narrative is not one of synthesis and blending, but of two distinct civilizationsâ€”Hindu and Muslimâ€”clashing under conditions of foreign political supremacy.
The central argument of this piece is that **the period of Indo-Islamic rule was fundamentally characterized by a foreign, military-dominated state that failed to achieve genuine national integration, maintaining a sustained level of political and religious antagonism with the majority population.**

II. Analysis: Developing the Argument

Jadunath Sarkar, through his meticulous studies of the Mughal Empire, viewed the state largely as a military occupation. He argued that the fundamental flaw lay in the **theocratic nature** of the state, which, even in its most liberal phases (like under Akbar), was inherently restrictive and fundamentally different from the inclusive character of Indian civilization.

A. Argument Point 1: The Character of the State and Religious Policy

Sarkar heavily focused on the reign of **Aurangzeb**, portraying it as the logical outcome of Islamic rule in India. Policies such as the re-imposition of the *jizya* tax and the destruction of temples are cited as definitive evidence of the state's intent to enforce religious hierarchy. Even rulers considered liberal were viewed with skepticism; Akbar's policies, while progressive, were seen as pragmatic measures to solidify political power rather than a true acceptance of religious pluralism. This structural intolerance, they argue, perpetually alienated the subject majority.

B. Argument Point 2: Persistent National Resistance and Lack of Synthesis

R. C. Majumdar emphasized the **absence of true national unity** during this period. For Majumdar, the numerous revolts, led by figures like the Rajputs, Sikhs, and particularly the Marathas under Shivaji, were not merely political rebellions but manifestations of a sustained **national and religious awakening** against foreign domination. He believed that despite superficial cultural borrowing, the two primary communities remained separate, with the Hindu population maintaining a defensive social posture. This continuous struggle prevented the emergence of a single, unified Indian polity, thus characterizing the era by conflict rather than compromise.

III. Conclusion: Synthesis and Implications

The historiography of Sarkar and Majumdar presents a clear-cut thesis: the centuries of Muslim rule in India were marked by **political conquest, religious persecution, and a failure of genuine cultural assimilation.** From this viewpoint, Hindu society survived this period largely due to its inherent resilience and ability to resist external pressures, not due to the benevolence of the ruling class. This interpretation, emphasizing conflict, heavily influenced early nationalist narratives and remains a potent lens through which many view the pre-modern era today.
